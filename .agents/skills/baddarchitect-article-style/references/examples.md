# Examples

These examples calibrate the house style. Do not copy them blindly; adapt the moves.

## Opening Move: Direct Point, Human Context, Leadership Tension

Strong pattern:

```markdown
AI adoption starts with **fear**.

People are paying attention. They see the headlines where executives talk about productivity, then watch companies fund AI programs while cutting teams elsewhere. They see entry-level hiring get tighter, support teams shrink, marketing teams reorganize, and software engineers wonder which parts of their craft will still matter.

In our experience, the successful programs acknowledged this fear **first**, created safe spaces for experimentation, and built trust through clear communication and visible action.
```

Why it works:

- The first line creates a clear thesis.
- The second paragraph gives the fear a rational basis.
- The third paragraph uses experience without overclaiming.
- Bold is used for the hinge concepts, not every interesting phrase.

## Message Contrast

Strong pattern:

```markdown
When leadership says, _"We want everyone to experiment with AI,"_ employees may hear two very different messages.

- _We want to help you learn the next important tool of your career._
- _We are collecting evidence that parts of your job can be automated._

Most leaders intend the first message. Many employees hear the second.

That gap is where trust is either built or lost.
```

Why it works:

- It respects leadership intent while naming employee anxiety.
- Bullets make the contrast visible.
- The one-liner at the end earns its place.

## Reader Questions

Strong pattern:

```markdown
People are not only afraid that AI will make mistakes. They are afraid of what AI **reveals** about how the company sees their work.

They wonder:

- Will this replace my job?
- Will my manager compare my output to an AI tool I was never trained to use?
- Will the fastest adopters be rewarded while careful people are labeled resistant?
- Will my private experiments become performance data?
- Will I be blamed when an AI-generated answer creates customer, security, or quality risk?
```

Why it works:

- The setup gives the questions emotional and operational meaning.
- The list mixes job fear, incentives, surveillance, and accountability.
- The questions are not melodramatic; they are plausible.

## Before And After: Too Generic To House Style

Before:

```markdown
Organizations need to create psychological safety for AI adoption. Leaders should communicate clearly and encourage experimentation while ensuring employees understand policies and governance requirements.
```

After:

```markdown
Safe AI adoption does not mean nobody feels challenged.

It means the rules are clear enough that people can participate without guessing the hidden consequences.

Employees need to know what tools are approved, what data is off limits, how experiments will be evaluated, and whether AI usage data can become performance data.

Without that clarity, leaders do not get adoption.

They get theater.
```

What changed:

- Abstract language became concrete.
- The reader sees the employee's risk, not just a leadership principle.
- The final one-liner sharpens the consequence.

## Before And After: Too Many One-Liners

Before:

```markdown
AI adoption starts with fear.

Not because people are irrational.

Because they are paying attention.

They see the headlines.

They hear the productivity talk.

They notice the layoffs.
```

After:

```markdown
AI adoption starts with **fear**.

People are not irrational. They are paying attention. They see executives talk about productivity while companies fund AI programs, reorganize teams, and tighten hiring elsewhere.

That context changes how every adoption message lands.
```

What changed:

- The opening still has force.
- The rhythm breathes instead of chopping every idea into a separate line.
- The final sentence turns the context into the article's operating problem.

## Wrong Leadership Move

Strong pattern:

```markdown
I have seen the worst version of AI adoption, and it sounds upbeat on the surface:

_Everyone should be using AI. Find five ways to use it this week. Share your prompts. Show us your productivity gains._

Most leaders mean well. They want motion. They want people to move past abstract debate and get hands-on.

But if they have not built **trust**, laid out a concrete **plan**, or explained how participation data will be used, the message lands differently.

- To the anxious employee, it sounds like a test.
- To the security-minded employee, it sounds like unmanaged risk.
- To the experienced employee, it sounds like the company is compressing years of judgment into a tool it has not learned to trust.
- To the enthusiastic employee, it can sound like permission to automate anything they can reach.
```

Why it works:

- It challenges the move without attacking the leader.
- It uses personal experience carefully.
- The bullet list shows different readers in the same room.

## Evidence Bridge

Strong pattern:

```markdown
These are not fringe concerns. They are normal concerns inside a serious transition.

The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs research frames technological change as one of the forces expected to reshape jobs and skills. Gallup's workplace research points to the adoption side of the same problem: employees use AI more when tools fit existing systems, managers support experimentation, and clear policies exist.

That last point matters.

Access is not adoption.

Adoption requires a **believable** environment.
```

Why it works:

- Evidence supports the practical claim without overwhelming the section.
- The interpretation is stronger than the citation summary.
- The one-liners land because they follow evidence and context.

## Conclusion Cadence

Strong pattern:

```markdown
AI adoption does not start with tooling.

It starts with trust.

Not blind trust in AI. Trust between people: leaders, managers, teams, security reviewers, product owners, engineers, and the people whose work will change.

Safe participation is the first operating model because it gives the organization a way to move without lying to itself.

Then participation.

Then workflow.
```

Why it works:

- The close is memorable, but not motivational fluff.
- It restates the system problem.
- The short ending works because the article has already earned it.
