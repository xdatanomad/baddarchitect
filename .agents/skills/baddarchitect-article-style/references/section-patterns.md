# Section Patterns

Use these patterns as flexible structures, not templates to fill mechanically.

## Opening Tension

Best for articles that need executive or organizational empathy.

Shape:

1. Metadata and takeaway.
2. One direct opening sentence.
3. A short paragraph naming what readers or employees are actually seeing.
4. A leadership message or common assumption.
5. The two ways that message can be heard.
6. The operating consequence if leaders leave the gap unaddressed.

Good when the article starts from fear, trust, ROI pressure, security concern, or adoption friction.

Avoid opening with broad history, industry hype, or "AI is changing everything."

## Signal Section

Best for grounding a claim in external evidence.

Shape:

1. Name the pattern in the market.
2. Cite one to three credible sources.
3. Explain the tension in the project's language.
4. Convert the signal into a design implication.

Useful section titles:

- `## The Signal`
- `## The Pressure Is Real`
- `## The Adoption Gap`
- `## What The Market Is Telling Us`

Keep the source summary short. The reader came for interpretation and operating guidance.

## Personal Example

Best for credibility when the article risks sounding abstract.

Shape:

1. "I have seen..." or "In our experience..."
2. Concrete situation: team, workflow, pressure, decision.
3. The moment of tension.
4. What the team learned or changed.
5. The broader lesson.

Rules:

- Use real experience only.
- Keep details generic if confidentiality matters.
- Do not present anecdotes as proof.
- Do not let the story exceed the point.

## Contrast Section

Best for clarifying a misunderstood concept.

Shape:

```markdown
The weak version sounds like this:

_Message or behavior._

The stronger version is:

_Message or behavior._

The difference is not tone. It is operating clarity.
```

Use for:

- Safe vs. comfortable
- Demo vs. workflow
- Output trust vs. operational trust
- Tool usage vs. transformation
- Personal productivity vs. production workflow

## Reader Questions

Best for drawing the reader into the problem.

Use bullets when the questions are emotionally or operationally distinct:

```markdown
Employees wonder:

- Will this replace my job?
- Will my private experiments become performance data?
- Will careful people be labeled resistant?
- Who is accountable if AI creates risk?
```

Avoid giant lists. Seven strong questions are usually better than fifteen similar ones.

## Operating Model Or Compact

Best when the article needs to move from diagnosis to action.

Shape:

1. Name the compact, gate, map, or model.
2. Explain what it is not.
3. Give numbered principles or steps.
4. Keep each item short, concrete, and observable.
5. Close with the operating reason it matters.

Examples:

- Participation compact
- Customer exposure gate
- Workflow readiness map
- AI value equation
- Internal automation launch gate

## Checklist Or Gate

Best for technical or decision-oriented articles.

Shape:

1. State when to use the checklist.
2. Ask precise questions.
3. Include ownership, data, permissions, measurement, failure mode, and human review where relevant.
4. End with a go/no-go rule.

Strong checklist questions:

- What event starts the workflow?
- What business metric should improve?
- What data and tools does the system touch?
- What should remain deterministic code?
- What happens on uncertainty or failure?
- Who owns the result after AI touches it?

## Conclusion

The close should compress the article into a useful operating belief.

Good conclusion movement:

1. Restate the thesis in plain language.
2. Name the false shortcut.
3. Name the durable operating move.
4. End with a short, memorable cadence.

Avoid:

- Motivational slogans.
- New evidence or new frameworks.
- Overly broad predictions.
- Repeating the full article in summary form.
